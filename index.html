Overview

What is a digital carbon footprint?

It’s the total greenhouse gases created by all the digital things we use: manufacturing and powering phones, laptops and routers; building and cooling data centres; and moving data across global networks while we browse, scroll, game, or stream.

Global share
3–4 %

Digital vs. the world

Estimates suggest that the information and communication technology (ICT) sector – including devices, networks, and data centres – generates around 3–4% of global greenhouse gas emissions, and this share is rising as more services move online.

That’s similar to or higher than the climate impact of aviation.

Everyday actions
Small but constant

Why habits matter

The impact of a single email or video is modest, but billions of people are online every day. Always-on devices, endless backups, autoplay videos and unused apps add up to huge demand for electricity and hardware.

How online habits affect the Earth
Streaming & video platforms ›

Video streaming is one of the heaviest uses of data. Each hour of HD video can cause on the order of tens of grams of CO₂, mainly through the electricity used by your device, home router, network equipment, and the data centre hosting the content.

  • Autoplay and background streaming keep traffic going even when nobody is watching.
  • Huge back-catalogues of shows, often stored in multiple copies, require storage and cooling.
  • Higher resolutions (4K, 8K) move more data, increasing energy use when networks are inefficient.
Emails, chats & online work Tiny × billions ›

A single short email might emit around a fraction of a gram to a few grams of CO₂, depending on attachments and how many people receive it. That sounds negligible, but globally we send and store hundreds of billions of messages.

  • Large attachments and images increase data transfer and storage needs.
  • Old messages and unused newsletters still live on servers drawing energy.
  • Reply-all culture multiplies the footprint of every message.
Cloud storage & “digital hoarding” ›

Photos, backups and forgotten files are stored in giant data centres. These facilities need servers, cooling, and backup power. Keeping everything forever means more hardware and more electricity over time.

  • Duplicate photos, unused backups and old projects keep servers busy 24/7.
  • “Dark data” – information that is stored but never used – increases emissions without adding value.
  • When hardware is replaced, manufacturing and e-waste add extra environmental pressure.
Devices & upgrades Hidden impact ›

A big part of a device’s lifetime emissions is locked in before you even unbox it. Mining metals, manufacturing chips and shipping laptops and phones all require large amounts of energy and resources.

  • Keeping devices longer often saves more emissions than any software tweak.
  • Repairing or refurbishing spreads manufacturing emissions over more years.
  • Recycling recovers some materials but can’t erase the original footprint.
AI, gaming & emerging tech ›

Newer digital services like large-scale AI, cloud gaming and crypto can consume huge amounts of computing power. Their impact depends heavily on how efficient the hardware is and how clean the electricity grid is.

  • Training and running large AI models requires powerful data-centre hardware.
  • Cloud gaming moves heavy graphics processing out of your console and into the cloud.
  • Policies, efficiency improvements and renewable energy are key to keeping growth in check.

Small actions, real impact

Stream a little smarter

Turn off autoplay, download favourites instead of repeatedly streaming them, and use Wi-Fi instead of mobile data when possible. Watching on a smaller device (phone or laptop) usually uses less power than a big TV.

Clean up your digital clutter

Regularly delete unused files, old backups, duplicate photos and newsletters you never read. Think of it as spring-cleaning your corner of the cloud.

Love your devices longer

Use devices for as many years as you can, repair when possible, and buy second-hand or refurbished hardware if it meets your needs. Fewer upgrades mean fewer factories running and less e-waste.
